Understanding Cepstral Peak Prominence (CPP): ASHA's Recommended Standard for Voice Assessment

November 2, 2025 ‚Ä¢
By Dr. Jorge C. Lucero
üìñ 12 minute read

üéØ Key Takeaways

  • ASHA recommends CPP as the primary acoustic measure for dysphonia assessment
  • CPP works where traditional measures fail: severely dysphonic voices, continuous speech, no pitch tracking needed
  • Clinical cutoffs (software-specific): For Praat/Phonalab CPP: <14.45 dB (vowel) or <9.33 dB (speech) suggests dysphonia
  • Clinical advantage: More robust correlation with perceived dysphonia severity
  • You can calculate CPP instantly with our free analyzer tool

In recent years, Cepstral Peak Prominence (CPP) has emerged as the most robust acoustic measure we have for assessing voice quality. The 2018 ASHA expert panel specifically recommended CPP as the primary acoustic measure for dysphonia assessment, replacing traditional perturbation measures like jitter and shimmer. Yet many clinicians still reach for those traditional measures; measures that often fail precisely when we need them most: with severely dysphonic voices.

If you were trained on jitter and shimmer, you're not alone. Most speech-language pathology programs still emphasize these traditional measures. But the field has shifted, and understanding CPP is now essential for evidence-based voice assessment.

Let me explain what CPP actually measures, why it's become ASHA's recommended standard, and, most importantly, how to use it effectively in your clinical practice, including which free tools are available and how to interpret software-specific values correctly.

The Problem with Traditional Measures

For decades, jitter (cycle-to-cycle pitch variability) and shimmer (cycle-to-cycle amplitude variability) were the go-to acoustic measures. They made intuitive sense: a smooth, periodic voice signal should have minimal perturbation, while a rough or hoarse voice would show increased variability.

But there's a fundamental problem: both measures require accurate fundamental frequency (F0) tracking.

‚ö†Ô∏è The Catch-22 of Perturbation Measures:

Jitter and shimmer work beautifully for mildly dysphonic voices, but fail catastrophically for severely dysphonic voices where you need objective measures most. When the voice becomes buried in noise (severe breathiness, roughness, or hoarseness), there isn't enough periodic information for the algorithm to identify individual vocal fold cycles.

This creates a clinical nightmare: your most severe patients, those who most need quantifiable evidence of disorder, are the ones whose voices can't be reliably measured with traditional methods.

Additional limitations of jitter and shimmer:

  • Valid only for sustained vowels: Can't assess voice quality during continuous speech, which is how patients actually communicate
  • Weak correlation with perception: The numbers don't always match what clinicians hear perceptually
  • High variability: Small changes in recording conditions or analysis settings can produce very different results
  • Limited clinical utility: Knowing jitter is 2.5% vs. 1.5% doesn't directly guide treatment decisions

Enter Cepstral Peak Prominence (CPP)

CPP emerged from signal processing research as a more robust alternative. The American Speech-Language-Hearing Association (ASHA) 2018 expert panel specifically recommended CPP as the primary acoustic measure for voice assessment, particularly for dysphonic voices. This recommendation remains current as of 2025.

What is CPP?

Without getting too technical: CPP measures the prominence of harmonic structure in the voice signal relative to background noise. Think of it as quantifying how "clear" the harmonic peaks are in the voice spectrum.

Conceptual Framework: Think of the voice spectrum as a city skyline. In a healthy voice, harmonic peaks (the buildings) stand tall and distinct above the noise floor (the horizon). In dysphonia, increased aperiodic energy (fog) obscures these harmonics. CPP quantifies how prominently the harmonic structure rises above this noise floor.

Clinical Cutoffs (Software-Specific)

CPP vs. CPPS - Critical Distinction: Modern tools like Praat and Phonalab calculate CPPS (smoothed CPP), which averages measurements across multiple time windows for more stable results. CPPS values are typically 3-4 dB higher than unsmoothed CPP (used by ADSV). The cutoffs below are software-specific; always compare your patient's values to cutoffs from the SAME software.

For Praat/Phonalab (CPP Smoothed):

  • Sustained /a/ vowel: Values <14.45 dB suggest voice disorder
  • Continuous speech (e.g., Rainbow Passage): Values <9.33 dB suggest voice disorder

For ADSV (CPP Unsmoothed):

  • Sustained /a/ vowel: Values <11.46 dB suggest voice disorder
  • Continuous speech: Values <6.11 dB suggest voice disorder

What Affects CPP?

Several factors influence CPP measurements:

  • Speech task: Continuous speech typically shows CPP 1-2 dB lower than sustained vowels
  • Loudness (SPL): Louder voice generally increases CPP
  • Pitch (F0): Higher pitches may show different CPP values
  • Age and gender: Some studies show slight differences, but less dramatic than with jitter/shimmer
  • Recording quality: While more robust than jitter/shimmer, poor recordings still affect CPP

How to Implement CPP in Your Practice

1. Choose Your Software

If you're on a budget or starting out: Use Praat. It's free, validated, although the interface isn't clinician-friendly.

If you have budget and see many voice patients: ADSV (Pentax Medical) offers streamlined workflow and built-in clinical reports, but requires significant investment ($10K-$30K+).

If you want the easiest option: Try Phonalab's free analyzer. It calculates CPP automatically with AI interpretation and PDF reports.

2. Standardize Your Recording Protocol (ASHA 2018)

ASHA-Recommended Protocol:
  • Microphone distance: 4-10 cm from mouth (NOT 10-15 cm as sometimes stated)
  • Positioning: 45¬∞-90¬∞ off-axis to reduce aerodynamic noise
  • Environment: Quiet room (background noise <50 dB)
  • Tasks: Sustained /a/ vowel (3-5 seconds) AND continuous speech (e.g., Rainbow Passage)
  • Intensity: Comfortable, habitual speaking volume
  • Consistency: Use same setup, software, and protocol for all patients

3. Track Trends, Not Absolute Numbers

CPP is most valuable for documenting change over time. A patient's baseline CPP of 8.2 dB increasing to 10.8 dB after therapy is meaningful; even if both values fall below typical normative ranges. What matters is the improvement trajectory.

4. Consider Multiparametric Analysis

No single measure tells the whole story. Modern approaches like the Acoustic Voice Quality Index (AVQI) combine CPP with other parameters for more comprehensive assessment. Our AVQI tool makes this easy.

5. Educate Your Patients

Visual feedback is powerful. Show patients their CPP improving over time using simple language: "This number measures how clear your voice signal is. We want to see it increase as your voice improves. You started at 7.5, and now you're at 10.2; that's excellent progress!"

When to Use CPP vs. Traditional Measures

Use CPP When:

  • Assessing moderate to severe dysphonia
  • You want to analyze continuous speech (more ecologically valid)
  • Working with breathy or rough voice qualities
  • Tracking treatment progress across time
  • Recording conditions aren't perfectly controlled (e.g., telehealth)
  • You need a measure that correlates well with perceptual severity
  • Following ASHA 2018 evidence-based guidelines

Traditional Measures Still Have Limited Use:

  • Mild, periodic dysphonia where F0 tracking is reliable
  • Research protocols requiring historical comparison
  • Specific clinical questions about vocal fold vibration regularity
  • As supplementary measures alongside CPP (multiparametric approach)
Best Practice Recommendation: Use CPP as your primary acoustic measure, supplemented by traditional measures when appropriate. This gives you the best of both worlds: robustness when you need it, plus additional detail when the voice signal allows.

Telehealth and Smartphone Recording Validation

Smartphone recordings show excellent correlation (r > 0.90) with clinical-grade equipment for CPP analysis, particularly when following standardized protocols.

Evidence-Based Recommendations for Remote Recording:

  • Device: Modern smartphone (iPhone or Android, last 3 years)
  • Distance: 4-10 cm (approximately 1.5-4 inches) from built-in microphone
  • Environment: Quiet room, no echo
  • Position: Slight off-axis (not directly into mic) to reduce breath noise
  • Format: Highest quality recording setting available (uncompressed if possible, or high-bitrate AAC/MP3)
  • Consistency: Use the same device and recording app for all sessions with a patient
  • Validation: Ask patient to record a brief test sample first to verify recording quality before proceeding
Research Update: The NIH-funded Bridge2AI-Voice consortium validated that tablet recordings with headset microphones at 2.5-5 cm show correlations >0.90 with research-grade equipment for CPP and other acoustic measures. This supports viable telehealth voice assessment when standardized protocols are followed.

The Physiological Mystery: What Does CPP Actually Measure?

Here's where I need to be honest with you: we don't fully understand the physiological basis of CPP yet.

With jitter and shimmer, the connection to vocal fold vibration is intuitive: irregular oscillation = irregular acoustics. But CPP is more abstract. It reflects the overall "harmonicity" of the voice, but what specific aspects of vocal fold function contribute to this?

Current hypotheses suggest CPP reflects:

  • Glottal closure completeness
  • Regularity of mucosal wave propagation
  • Ratio of periodic to aperiodic energy (signal-to-noise)
  • Overall coordination of laryngeal and respiratory systems

This remains an active area of research as of 2025. What we know for certain is that CPP correlates strongly with perceptual voice quality ratings (r = 0.60-0.85) and clinical outcomes, even if the precise physiological mechanisms remain unclear.

‚ö†Ô∏è Clinical Implication:

Because we don't fully understand the physiology, CPP improvements don't tell us exactly which physiological changes occurred. A patient might achieve better CPP through improved closure, reduced edema, or better respiratory support. This is why CPP should complement, not replace, perceptual assessment and laryngoscopy.

Common Questions About CPP

Q: Can I calculate CPP with free software?

Yes! Praat is free although it has a learning curve and counter intuitive interface. Download it at https://osf.io/t5hrv/. Our Phonalab analyzer also provides free CPP calculation with AI interpretation and PDF reports.

Q: Why are my Praat or Phonalab values higher than values I see in papers?

This is normal! Praat and Phonalab calculates CPPS (smoothed CPP), which is typically 3-4 dB higher than the CPP from ADSV. Papers using different software report different ranges. Always check which software was used and apply software-matched normative values.

Q: Is CPP valid for telehealth recordings?

Yes, with proper protocols. Recent research shows smartphone recordings achieve correlations >0.90 with clinical equipment for CPP when standardized methods are followed. Key factors: quiet environment, consistent device/distance, high-quality recording settings. CPP is particularly robust across recording methods compared to jitter/shimmer.

Q: Should I report CPP to referring physicians?

Absolutely. Many ENTs are familiar with CPP from the literature. Include a brief interpretation: "CPP of 8.2 dB indicates moderate dysphonia; values >14 dB are typical for healthy voice." Always specify which software you used.

Q: How does CPP relate to the Voice Handicap Index (VHI)?

Moderate correlation (r = 0.4-0.6). This is expected and normal. CPP captures acoustic/physiological impairment, while VHI reflects patient-perceived handicap. Both are valuable; use them together for comprehensive assessment. A patient might have poor CPP but low VHI (compensating well) or good CPP but high VHI (significant impact despite mild acoustic impairment).

The Future: AI and Voice Analysis

The field is experiencing a paradigm shift toward AI-driven voice analysis. While CPP remains the current gold standard, exciting developments are emerging:

Machine Learning Integration

  • Deep Learning models: CNNs and transformer architectures analyzing voice spectrograms directly, achieving diagnostic accuracy comparable to human experts
  • Multi-disease detection: AI models detecting voice changes associated with Parkinson's, COVID-19, cognitive decline, and other systemic conditions
  • Automated feature extraction: ML discovering acoustic patterns beyond traditional parameters
  • Clinical decision support: AI-assisted interpretation combining acoustic, perceptual, and patient-reported data

Current AI approaches incorporate CPP as one feature among many rather than replacing it. The future likely involves hybrid approaches combining traditional acoustic parameters like CPP with AI-discovered features.

Bottom Line: Why CPP Matters for Your Practice

Here's what you need to remember:

  1. CPP is ASHA's recommended primary acoustic measure for dysphonia assessment (2018 guidelines, still current in 2025)
  2. SOFTWARE MATTERS CRITICALLY: Praat/Phonalab CPPS values are 3-4 dB higher than ADSV CPP; always use software-specific norms
  3. Clinical cutoffs (software-specific):
    • Praat/Phonalab CPPS: <14.45 dB (vowel) or <9.33 dB (speech)
    • ADSV CPP: <11.46 dB (vowel) or <6.11 dB (speech)
  4. Track trends over time rather than fixating on single values
  5. Use alongside perceptual assessment and laryngoscopy for comprehensive evaluation
  6. Perfect for telehealth due to robustness to recording variability (when protocols followed)
  7. Works where jitter/shimmer fail: moderate-to-severe dysphonia, continuous speech

The shift from jitter/shimmer to CPP isn't just about adopting a new measure; it's about embracing more robust, clinically meaningful voice assessment that works when you need it most.
